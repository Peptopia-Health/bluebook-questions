1 🩸 Antepartum Hemorrhage
⚠️ Placenta Previa VS 🚨 Placental Abruption
Feature ⚠️ Placenta Previa 🚨 Placental Abruption
Definition Placenta implants over or near the internal cervical os — partially or completely covering it Premature separation of a normally implanted placenta from the uterine wall before delivery
Hallmark Sign Painless, bright red vaginal bleeding — typically in 2nd or 3rd trimester Painful, dark red bleeding with rigid "board-like" abdomen. May be concealed (no visible bleeding)
Uterine Tone Soft, relaxed, non-tender Rigid, board-like, tender (tetanic contractions)
Bleeding Bright red, episodic, painless. Amount does NOT correlate with degree of previa Dark red. May be concealed (20% of abruptions) — no visible bleeding but fundal height increases and shock develops
Fetal Status Usually normal unless maternal hemorrhage is severe Often compromised — fetal distress, IUGR, or fetal death depending on severity
Risk Factors Prior C-section, prior previa, multiparity, advanced maternal age, smoking, uterine surgery Hypertension (#1 risk), cocaine use, trauma (MVA, abuse), prior abruption, polyhydramnios, short cord

💊 Management Stable, preterm: bed rest, no vaginal exams, monitor for bleeding, betamethasone if <34 wks
Active bleeding or ≥36 wks: cesarean delivery		 Mild: close monitoring, bed rest, fetal surveillance
Moderate-severe: emergent delivery (vaginal if stable, C-section if fetal distress)
Treat for DIC if develops
Delivery Cesarean section — vaginal delivery is contraindicated for complete previa Depends on severity and fetal status — may be vaginal or cesarean. Emergent delivery if fetal distress.
NEVER perform a digital vaginal exam on a patient with suspected placenta previa! Palpating through the cervix can rupture placental vessels and cause catastrophic hemorrhage. Always confirm placental location by ultrasound first. This is a classic NCLEX question.
NCLEX Key — The #1 Differentiator: Painless bright red bleeding = think Placenta Previa (P = Painless). Painful dark red bleeding with a rigid abdomen = think Placental Abruption (A = Agonizing). This mnemonic alone answers most NCLEX questions on antepartum hemorrhage.

Vasa Previa

🩸 Vasa Previa — Rare but Lethal

FETAL EMERGENCY

Definition: Fetal blood vessels run through the membranes across the internal cervical os, unprotected by placental tissue or the umbilical cord.

  • Classic presentation: Rupture of membranes → painless vaginal bleeding + sudden fetal bradycardia/death
  • The blood is fetal (not maternal) — even small amounts can be lethal to the fetus
  • Diagnosis: Prenatal ultrasound with color Doppler. Apt test (alkali denaturation test) differentiates fetal from maternal blood
  • Management: Planned cesarean at 35-36 weeks before labor begins. If membranes rupture → immediate emergency C-section
  • Fetal mortality: >60% if undiagnosed, but <5% if diagnosed prenatally
2 🚨 Labor & Delivery Emergencies

🔴 Umbilical Cord Prolapse

OBSTETRIC EMERGENCY

Definition: Umbilical cord descends ahead of or alongside the fetal presenting part after rupture of membranes, causing cord compression and fetal hypoxia.

Immediate Nursing Actions:

  1. Call for help — this requires an emergency team response
  2. Insert gloved hand into vagina and lift the presenting part OFF the cord — maintain continuous manual pressure
  3. Place patient in knee-chest position or steep Trendelenburg — uses gravity to relieve cord compression
  4. Do NOT attempt to replace the cord into the uterus
  5. If cord is protruding, keep it warm and moist with sterile saline-soaked towels — do NOT handle it
  6. Administer O2 at 8-10 L/min via non-rebreather
  7. Prepare for immediate emergency cesarean section
  8. Continuous fetal heart rate monitoring — expect variable decelerations

Risk factors: Polyhydramnios, breech/transverse lie, premature rupture of membranes, multiparity, unengaged presenting part, long umbilical cord

NCLEX Key: The priority for cord prolapse is to relieve pressure on the cord. The nurse's hand stays in the vagina lifting the presenting part until the baby is delivered by emergency C-section. This is one of the few situations where the nurse maintains direct contact with the patient continuously until surgery.

⚠️ Shoulder Dystocia

EMERGENCY DELIVERY COMPLICATION

Definition: The fetal head delivers but the anterior shoulder becomes impacted behind the maternal pubic symphysis. Creates a "turtle sign" — head retracts against the perineum.

McRoberts Maneuver — First-Line Intervention:

  • Hyperflex maternal thighs sharply against the abdomen — this straightens the sacrum and increases the pelvic outlet
  • Apply suprapubic pressure (NOT fundal pressure!) — firm downward pressure above the pubic bone to dislodge the anterior shoulder
  • If unsuccessful: Wood's screw maneuver (rotate the posterior shoulder), deliver posterior arm, or move to all-fours (Gaskin maneuver)

Risk factors: Fetal macrosomia (>4000g), gestational diabetes, maternal obesity, prolonged 2nd stage, prior shoulder dystocia

Complications: Brachial plexus injury (Erb's palsy), clavicle fracture, fetal hypoxia, maternal lacerations, postpartum hemorrhage

NEVER apply fundal pressure during shoulder dystocia! Fundal pressure further impacts the shoulder against the pubic bone and can cause uterine rupture. Only suprapubic pressure is safe. This is an absolute NCLEX rule.

🔴 Uterine Rupture

LIFE-THREATENING

Definition: Complete tear through the uterine wall, often at a previous cesarean scar site. The fetus may be partially or completely extruded into the abdominal cavity.

Classic Signs:

  • Sudden, severe "tearing" abdominal pain — often described as "something ripped"
  • Cessation of contractions — labor abruptly stops
  • Fetal parts palpable through abdomen (Bandl's ring sign before rupture)
  • Signs of hemorrhagic shock — tachycardia, hypotension, decreased LOC
  • Fetal heart rate: Prolonged bradycardia or absent FHR
  • Change in uterine shape and loss of fetal station

Management: Immediate laparotomy — deliver the baby, repair or remove the uterus (hysterectomy may be necessary). Massive transfusion protocol.

Risk factors: Prior classical (vertical) uterine incision (#1), VBAC trial, oxytocin augmentation, grand multiparity, uterine anomalies

🔴 Amniotic Fluid Embolism (AFE)

CATASTROPHIC HIGH MORTALITY

Definition: Amniotic fluid, fetal cells, or debris enter the maternal circulation, triggering a massive anaphylactoid reaction with cardiovascular collapse and DIC.

Classic Presentation — Sudden Triad:

  • Acute respiratory distress / dyspnea
  • Cardiovascular collapse / hypotension
  • DIC with massive hemorrhage

Onset: Typically during labor, delivery, or immediately postpartum. Often preceded by intense shivering and anxiety.

Mortality: 60-80% mortality rate — one of the most lethal obstetric complications. Survivors often have neurological deficits.

Treatment: Supportive care only — intubation, vasopressors, blood products for DIC, immediate delivery if undelivered. No specific treatment exists.

3 📊 Fetal Heart Rate Monitoring
Normal FHR Baseline: 110-160 bpm with moderate variability (6-25 bpm fluctuation). This is a Category I tracing — reassuring and requires no intervention.

Accelerations & Decelerations

Pattern Shape / Timing Cause Significance
Accelerations ↑ 15 bpm above baseline for ≥15 seconds Fetal movement, stimulation Reassuring — indicates intact CNS. Required for a reactive NST (2 accels in 20 min)
Early Decels Gradual ↓ that mirrors contractions (onset, nadir, and recovery match contraction timing) Head compression during contractions (vagal response) Benign — normal labor finding. No intervention needed. Early = hEad
Late Decels Gradual ↓ that starts AFTER contraction peak and recovers AFTER contraction ends (offset from contraction) Uteroplacental insufficiency — decreased O2 transfer to fetus Ominous! Indicates fetal hypoxia. Late = pLacenta. Intervene immediately!
Variable Decels Abrupt ↓ (onset to nadir <30 sec), variable shape and timing — look like "V", "W", or "U" Cord compression Most common decel type. Mild = monitor. Severe/recurrent with poor variability = concerning. Variable = Cord (VC)
Prolonged Decels ↓ ≥15 bpm lasting 2-10 minutes Cord prolapse, uterine rupture, maternal hypotension, tachysystole Emergency if >3 min. Requires immediate assessment and intervention
Memory Aid — "VEAL CHOP":
Variable decels = Cord compression
Early decels = Head compression
Accelerations = OK (reassuring)
Late decels = Placental insufficiency

Category Classification System

CategoryCharacteristicsAction
Category I Baseline 110-160, moderate variability, no late/variable decels, accelerations present or absent Normal — continue routine monitoring
Category II Anything NOT Cat I or Cat III — includes: minimal variability, recurrent variable decels, prolonged decels, tachycardia/bradycardia Indeterminate — requires evaluation, continued monitoring, possible intervention. Most tracings fall here.
Category III Absent variability WITH recurrent late decels, recurrent variable decels, or bradycardia. OR sinusoidal pattern Abnormal — requires IMMEDIATE intervention. Prepare for emergent delivery

Intrauterine Resuscitation — Interventions for Non-Reassuring FHR

📊 Intrauterine Resuscitation Steps

KNOW THIS SEQUENCE
  1. Reposition maternal patient — left lateral, then right lateral, then hands-and-knees. Avoid supine (vena cava compression)
  2. Administer O2 at 8-10 L/min via non-rebreather mask
  3. IV fluid bolus — lactated Ringer's or normal saline to increase maternal blood volume and placental perfusion
  4. Discontinue oxytocin if infusing — eliminates drug-induced tachysystole
  5. If tachysystole: administer terbutaline 0.25 mg SQ to relax the uterus
  6. Amnioinfusion for recurrent variable decelerations (instill warm saline into uterus to cushion cord)
  7. Notify provider and prepare for possible operative delivery if pattern does not improve
NCLEX Key: When you see late decelerations on the monitor, your first action is to reposition the mother (left lateral). Then give O2, IV fluids, and stop oxytocin. If the question asks "which action is MOST important?" — position change is usually the answer because it's immediately actionable and addresses the root cause (placental perfusion).
4 💜 Postpartum Complications

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

🩸 Postpartum Hemorrhage

LEADING CAUSE OF MATERNAL DEATH

Definition: Blood loss ≥1000 mL or bleeding with signs of hypovolemia within 24 hours of delivery (early PPH) or 24 hours–12 weeks postpartum (late PPH).

The "4 T's" of PPH Causes:
Tone — Uterine atony (#1 cause — 80% of PPH)
Trauma — Lacerations, hematomas, uterine inversion/rupture
Tissue — Retained placenta or placental fragments
Thrombin — Coagulopathy (DIC, von Willebrand's, anticoagulant use)
CauseAssessment FindingsInterventions
Uterine Atony Boggy, soft, non-contracted uterus. Fundus above expected level. Steady flow of dark blood. May not appear dramatic until significant volume lost. Bimanual uterine massage (first-line)
Oxytocin 20-40 units in 1L NS IV
Methylergonovine (Methergine) 0.2mg IM
Carboprost (Hemabate) 0.25mg IM
Misoprostol 800-1000mcg rectally
Bakri balloon tamponade if meds fail
Trauma Firm uterus with continued bright red bleeding. Inspect for lacerations, hematomas. Signs of uterine inversion (fundus not palpable, mass in vagina) Surgical repair of lacerations
Uterine inversion: do NOT remove placenta until repositioned; manual repositioning or surgical correction
Tissue Incomplete placenta on inspection, uterus cannot fully contract. May present as late PPH (days-weeks later) Manual removal of retained fragments
Uterine curettage (D&C)
Ultrasound to confirm retained products
Thrombin Bleeding from multiple sites (IV sites, gums), oozing from incisions, blood does not clot. Low fibrinogen, elevated PT/PTT, elevated D-dimer Treat underlying cause
Blood product replacement: FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets
Massive transfusion protocol if needed
Methylergonovine (Methergine) is CONTRAINDICATED in hypertension! It causes intense vasoconstriction and can trigger stroke, MI, or hypertensive crisis. Always check BP before administering. If the patient is preeclamptic or hypertensive → use carboprost or misoprostol instead. This is a guaranteed NCLEX question.
Carboprost (Hemabate) is CONTRAINDICATED in asthma! It causes bronchospasm. Remember: Methergine = no hypertension, Hemabate = no asthma.

Other Postpartum Complications

ComplicationKey FeaturesManagement
Endometritis Fever (>100.4°F/38°C), foul-smelling lochia, uterine tenderness, tachycardia. Usually 2-5 days postpartum. Risk: C-section, prolonged ROM, multiple vaginal exams IV clindamycin + gentamicin (gold standard). Continue until afebrile 24-48h
DVT / PE DVT: Unilateral leg swelling, warmth, redness, positive Homans sign (unreliable). PE: Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, hypoxia, anxiety Heparin anticoagulation (safe for breastfeeding). PE: Supplemental O2, CT angiography for diagnosis, heparin → warfarin transition
PP Depression Persistent sadness, hopelessness, sleep/appetite changes, withdrawal from baby, loss of interest. Onset: typically 2 weeks–6 months postpartum Screening: Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale. Treatment: therapy, SSRIs (safe in breastfeeding: sertraline, paroxetine)
PP Psychosis Psychiatric emergency. Hallucinations, delusions, severe confusion, disorganized behavior. Onset: first 2 weeks. Risk of infanticide/suicide Psychiatric admission. Never leave mother alone with infant. Antipsychotics, mood stabilizers. Report if safety risk to infant.
NCLEX Key — PP Depression vs PP Psychosis: PP depression = sad, withdrawn, may have trouble bonding. PP psychosis = hallucinations, delusions, risk of harm to self/infant. PP psychosis is a psychiatric emergency requiring immediate intervention and separation of mother and infant when unsupervised.

APGAR Scoring

APGAR Interpretation:
7-10 = Normal (most newborns score 8-9 at 1 minute due to acrocyanosis)
4-6 = Moderately depressed — stimulate, suction, possible O2
0-3 = Severely depressed — immediate resuscitation (PPV, intubation, chest compressions)
NCLEX Key: APGAR is assessed at 1 minute (overall status) and 5 minutes (response to resuscitation). If the 5-minute score is <7, continue scoring every 5 minutes up to 20 minutes. APGAR does NOT guide resuscitation decisions — resuscitation begins immediately at birth if needed, NOT after waiting 1 minute for an APGAR score.

6 ⚠️ Newborn Complications
ComplicationKey FeaturesManagement
Hypoglycemia Blood glucose <40 mg/dL in first 4h, <45 after 4h. Signs: jitteriness, tremors, poor feeding, lethargy, seizures, hypothermia, weak cry.
At risk: LGA, SGA, IDM (infant of diabetic mother), preterm, cold stress		 Feed immediately if alert and able to suck (breast or formula). Recheck glucose 30 min after feeding. If unable to feed or glucose <25: D10W IV bolus 2 mL/kg then continuous infusion. Never use D25W or D50W in neonates (causes rebound hypoglycemia and vein sclerosis)
Jaundice Yellow skin/sclera from elevated bilirubin. Physiologic: appears after 24h, peaks day 3-5, resolves by 2 wks. Pathologic: appears within 24 hours — always abnormal!
Risk: ABO/Rh incompatibility, prematurity, bruising, polycythemia, breastfeeding insufficiency		 Phototherapy (bili lights) — expose maximum skin area, protect eyes with opaque mask, monitor temperature, increase feedings. Exchange transfusion if bilirubin levels are critically high or rising rapidly despite phototherapy
Kernicterus Unconjugated bilirubin crosses the blood-brain barrier → permanent brain damage. Signs: high-pitched cry, opisthotonos (arched back), absent Moro reflex, seizures, lethargy Prevention is key — aggressive phototherapy and exchange transfusion before critical levels. Kernicterus is irreversible once established.
Respiratory Distress Syndrome Preterm infants lacking surfactant. Signs: tachypnea, grunting, nasal flaring, intercostal/substernal retractions, cyanosis. Classic "ground glass" appearance on chest X-ray. Onset within first 6 hours Exogenous surfactant via endotracheal tube
CPAP or mechanical ventilation
Prevention: Betamethasone to mother 24-48h before preterm delivery
NAS (Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome) Withdrawal from maternal opioid use. Onset: 24-72h after birth. Signs: high-pitched cry, tremors, irritability, sneezing, poor feeding, diarrhea, sweating, seizures. Score with Finnegan Scale Swaddling, dim quiet environment, small frequent feedings. Pharmacologic: morphine or methadone taper if Finnegan score ≥8 on 3 consecutive assessments. Breastfeeding encouraged if mother is in treatment and HIV-negative
Cold Stress Hypothermia triggers increased metabolic rate → ↑O2 consumption, ↑glucose utilization → hypoglycemia, metabolic acidosis, respiratory distress. Neonates lose heat via: evaporation (wet skin), conduction (cold surfaces), convection (drafts), radiation (cold nearby surfaces) Skin-to-skin (kangaroo care), radiant warmer, knit cap, warm blankets, dry thoroughly at birth. Prevention is the priority — warm and dry immediately after delivery
NCLEX Key — Jaundice Timing: Jaundice within <24 hours of birth = ALWAYS pathologic (Rh/ABO incompatibility, sepsis, TORCH). Jaundice after 24 hours = likely physiologic but still monitor. This timing distinction is tested repeatedly.
7 💊 High-Risk OB Medications
DrugUseKey Nursing Considerations
Oxytocin (Pitocin) Induction/augmentation of labor; PPH prevention and treatment Always via IV pump — never bolus during labor. Monitor for tachysystole (>5 contractions in 10 min). Water intoxication is possible (antidiuretic effect) — monitor I&O, Na levels. Stop immediately if fetal distress or tachysystole. Antidote for tachysystole: terbutaline
Terbutaline Tocolytic (stops preterm labor); treats uterine tachysystole Beta-2 agonist → uterine relaxation. Side effects: tachycardia, tremor, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia. Dose: 0.25 mg SQ. Monitor maternal HR (hold if >120), glucose, K+. Contraindicated if maternal HR >120 bpm
Betamethasone Fetal lung maturity acceleration (24-34 weeks gestation) 2 doses of 12mg IM, 24 hours apart. Optimal effect at 48 hours after first dose. Crosses placenta → stimulates fetal surfactant production. Monitor maternal blood glucose (causes hyperglycemia). Can be given with other OB drugs.
RhoGAM (Rh Immunoglobulin) Prevents Rh sensitization in Rh-negative mothers Given at 28 weeks and within 72 hours of delivery of an Rh-positive infant. Also given after: miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, amniocentesis, abdominal trauma, placental abruption. IM injection only. Indirect Coombs test must be negative before administration.
Methylergonovine (Methergine) PPH treatment — sustained uterine contraction 0.2mg IM (can give PO for ongoing management). Causes intense uterine contraction AND vasoconstriction. CONTRAINDICATED in hypertension — check BP before giving. Never use to induce labor. Store in refrigerator.
Carboprost (Hemabate) PPH treatment — prostaglandin F2-alpha 0.25mg IM into muscle (can give intramyometrial). Causes intense uterine contraction + bronchospasm. CONTRAINDICATED in asthma. Side effects: diarrhea, N/V, fever. Max 8 doses.
Misoprostol (Cytotec) PPH (rectal), cervical ripening (vaginal/oral), medical abortion PPH dose: 800-1000 mcg rectally. Prostaglandin E1 analog. Cervical ripening: 25 mcg vaginal q4-6h. Remove before starting oxytocin — risk of uterine hyperstimulation. Contraindicated if prior C-section (cervical ripening use).
Magnesium Sulfate Seizure prophylaxis (preeclampsia/eclampsia), fetal neuroprotection (<32 wks), tocolysis See our OB Hypertensive Disorders guide for complete MgSO4 reference. Antidote: calcium gluconate. Monitor: DTRs, RR ≥12, UO ≥30 mL/hr. Therapeutic level: 4-7 mEq/L.
NCLEX Medication Safety Rules:
• Methergine = NO hypertension (vasoconstriction → stroke risk)
• Hemabate = NO asthma (bronchospasm)
• Misoprostol for cervical ripening = NO prior C-section (uterine rupture risk)
• Oxytocin = ALWAYS via pump, NEVER bolus during labor
• RhoGAM = within 72 hours (IM only, Coombs negative first)
These contraindications are tested on virtually every NCLEX exam.
8 🧠 High-Yield NCLEX Memory Anchors

🩸 Previa vs Abruption

  • Previa = Painless, bright red
  • Abruption = Agonizing, dark red, rigid
  • Previa: NO vaginal exam — US only
  • Abruption: #1 risk = hypertension

📊 VEAL CHOP

  • Variable = Cord
  • Early = Head (benign)
  • Accelerations = OK
  • Late = Placenta (ominous)

🩸 4 T's of PPH

  • Tone (atony — #1 cause, 80%)
  • Trauma (lacerations)
  • Tissue (retained placenta)
  • Thrombin (coagulopathy)

💊 PPH Med Contraindications

  • Methergine → NO hypertension
  • Hemabate → NO asthma
  • Misoprostol ripening → NO prior C/S
  • Oxytocin → ALWAYS via pump

🔴 Cord Prolapse

  • Hand in vagina → lift presenting part
  • Knee-chest or Trendelenburg
  • Keep cord warm & moist
  • Emergency C-section

👶 Newborn Red Flags

  • Jaundice <24h = ALWAYS pathologic
  • Grunting + flaring + retractions = RDS
  • High-pitched cry + tremors = NAS or hypoglycemia
  • No meconium by 48h = obstruction

💉 RhoGAM Timing

  • 28 weeks (routine)
  • Within 72 hours postpartum
  • After any bleeding event
  • IM only, Coombs negative first

🏥 PP Warning Signs

  • Boggy uterus = atony → massage
  • Foul lochia + fever = endometritis
  • Unilateral leg swelling = DVT
  • Hallucinations = PP psychosis (emergency!)
Final NCLEX Tip: OB questions test your ability to prioritize in emergencies. When in doubt: airway/safety first (maternal AND fetal), then identify the complication (use your comparison knowledge), then intervene (know your meds and their contraindications). Fetal monitoring interpretation and PPH management appear on nearly every NCLEX exam — master VEAL CHOP and the 4 T's and you'll have a strong foundation.
